What is a CPAP Machine?

CPAP machines treat sleep apnea by delivering continuous air pressure through a mask worn over the nose and/or mouth, keeping airways open during sleep. A CPAP machine consists of a mask, straps, a tube, a motor (which blows air), and an air filter. Some have added features like humidifiers. CPAP is the most common type of PAP (positive airway pressure) device. Other PAP machines include auto CPAP (self-regulating pressure), Bi-level PAP (two pressures - higher for inhalation, lower for exhalation), auto-bilevel PAP (self-regulating inhalation and exhalation pressures), and ASV (adaptive servo-ventilation) for central sleep apnea, which monitors breathing, adjusts pressure, and can deliver breaths.  

What is a CPAP Machine Used For?

CPAP machines are primarily used to treat obstructive sleep apnea (OSA). OSA causes repeated breathing pauses during sleep due to airway blockage, leading to oxygen deprivation and increasing the risk of heart disease, high blood pressure, stroke, type 2 diabetes, and cognitive impairment, as well as daytime sleepiness that increases the risk of accidents. CPAP keeps airways open, preventing these pauses. CPAP is also used for other breathing problems, such as in preterm infants with underdeveloped lungs.

What Does a CPAP Machine Do?

A CPAP machine filters and pressurizes room air, delivering it through a tube to a mask. This airflow keeps the tongue, uvula, and soft palate from obstructing the airway, stabilizing breathing and improving sleep quality. Consistent use during all sleep periods (home, travel, naps) is essential for effective treatment, along with daily cleaning of the mask and tube, and timely prescription refills for replacements.

Types of CPAP Machines

CPAP machines primarily differ based on mask type, which is chosen based on comfort, breathing habits, and sleep apnea type. Mask options include nasal masks (covering the nose, good for active sleepers), nasal pillow masks (covering only nostrils, compatible with glasses), full masks (covering nose and mouth, best for mouth breathers or nasal blockage), and hybrid masks (a full mask with prongs or cushions sealing the nostrils without covering the nasal bridge). Healthcare providers help patients choose the best mask type and fit, and often, patients try several masks before finding the most comfortable option.

Risks & Benefits

Consistent CPAP use for sleep apnea offers both short- and long-term benefits. Short-term benefits include improved sleep quality, reduced snoring, less daytime sleepiness, and improved mood. Long-term benefits may include improved blood pressure, reduced cardiovascular disease risk (heart attack, stroke), and improved cognitive function.
