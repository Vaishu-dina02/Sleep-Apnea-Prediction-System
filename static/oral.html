What are oral appliances for sleep apnea?

Oral appliances are dental devices, also known as mouthpieces, that are worn in the mouth during sleep to maintain an open airway, effectively treating obstructive sleep apnea (OSA). OSA occurs when the upper airway becomes partially or completely blocked during sleep, disrupting airflow. This blockage triggers the brain to rouse the individual, often without full awakening, just enough to resume breathing. This cycle of airway restriction, brief arousal, and falling back asleep repeats throughout the night, preventing restful sleep and depriving vital organs of sufficient oxygen. Untreated OSA can have severe consequences, contributing to the development of serious health conditions and posing a potential risk to life. Oral appliances address OSA by repositioning the jaw, tongue, or other oral structures, effectively holding the mouth in a position that ensures an adequate and consistent flow of air. This improved airflow facilitates better breathing patterns and significantly reduces the frequency of OSA-related awakenings, leading to more restful and restorative sleep.

Types of oral appliances used to treat obstructive sleep apnea (OSA)

There are two primary types of oral appliances for OSA: mandibular advancement devices (MADs) and tongue-stabilizing devices (TSDs). Mandibular Advancement Devices (MADs): The most common oral appliance for OSA, MADs (also called mandibular advancement splints, appliances, or repositioning appliances) work by moving the lower jaw (mandible) forward. This action also pulls the tongue forward, increasing airflow space at the back of the throat. MADs fit over the teeth, typically in two parts (upper and lower) connected by screws, hinges, or rubber bands that allow for adjustable advancement of the lower jaw. Some have independent interchangeable arches. MADs vary in size, material firmness/softness, connection method between the two pieces, teeth contact when the mouth is closed, and the extent of jaw advancement. Tongue-Stabilizing Devices (TSDs): Also known as tongue-retaining devices, TSDs treat OSA by using suction to pull the tongue forward. A suction bulb on the device holds the tongue in place during sleep, with the tip of the TSD remaining outside the mouth. Like MADs, TSDs open the airway by moving the tongue's base forward. TSDs are less common than MADs but can be an option for those with OSA who can't use MADs. For example, individuals with weakened teeth that can't support a MAD may opt for a TSD. TSDs are also available over the counter .

Risks & Benefits

Oral appliance therapy for OSA can cause short-term side effects during the initial adjustment period (1-2 weeks), including increased salivation, jaw/teeth/TMJ pain, dry mouth, and gum irritation. These symptoms usually subside with continued use. Long-term side effects are rare with properly fitted appliances but may include bite changes, teeth movement/loosening, and TMJ/jaw muscle changes. Regular dental visits are essential for monitoring dental health, ensuring proper appliance fit and maintenance, and addressing any issues. Patients should bring their appliances to all dental appointments.
